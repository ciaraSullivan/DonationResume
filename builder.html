Welcome to Resume Builder!

Build the perfect resume for your cherished item. The final product will be a versatile pdf or text document that you can send to any institution around the world.
Fill out as much of this form as possible. If you have questions along the way, refer back to this welcome screen or review the directions under each form section. Return to this welcome screen for more information. Clears the entire form for a new entry. Choose whether to download a PDF or TXT version of your resume right to your computer. View a preview of your resume. Tips:

  • Try to be as neat as possible in your writing, and fill in as much information as you think could be relevant.
  • None of the items on this tool are required, so feel free to leave sections blank if you aren’t sure or if you don’t think it applies.
  • This is meant to be an easy and efficient way to describe your donation to any institution you want to send it to, but it likely will not answer every question they may have about the item(s). It is expected that the institution may want to talk further and even meet with you to discuss it, this is only meant to speed up this process and give you as much knowledge as possible going into the discussion.
  • Researchers are paid to find the answers to a lot of these questions, so don’t worry if you don’t have all of the answers. Use your best judgment, be truthful if you aren’t certain.

Name

More information:

First and foremost, how can the institution get a hold of you? Do you prefer being contacted by phone or by email? The fields on this tool are meant to be flexible, so if you’re donating on behalf of a business or organization, it is fine to use that in the ‘Name’ section. Address
This section is up to your discretion. You do not have to provide your full address, but consider adding your city, state, and country at minimum. This will give institutions an idea of how far the item would need to be sent in the event that it is accepted into their collection.

Description

More information:

This section should serve as an overall description of your item(s). Name of the Item(s): If you were referring to this item, what would you call it?
ex. City Hall Photographs 1856, Andy Warhol “Campbell’s Soup Cans”, Michigan Tiger’s Glove Type of Item(s)
Single Item – one object, Collection of Items – multiple objects. Medium
This should be a general summary of what the item is. You’ll be able to go into more detail later. ex. Photographs, Screen Print, Baseball Glove Description
Later on you’ll be able to tell more specific details, like the story of the item (how you got it, any folklore surrounding it, etc.), the materials used, the condition, subject areas, and more. *You may choose to fill out the rest of the form first, then come back here to write a description later on.

Dimensions

More information:

Institutions would probably be willing to take anything you’re willing to give, but unfortunately they need to be able to store it. This information will help them be able to ship the item if necessary, store it, and display it. Even if you aren’t planning to ship anything, please fill out the item’s weight and dimensions to the best of your ability. Item Size and Weight
Be as accurate and detailed as possible. What is the maximum amount of space that this could take up? Make sure to select the units your using. If the dimensions of your item(s) are more complicated, feel free to describe them as best you can in the Description section. Shipping Information
This is an area to explain potential challenges in shipping or measuring your item. How is it fragile? Why aren’t you open to shipping it to an institution?

Condition

More information:

Describe the condition of the items in as much detail as possible. You will be able to add pictures and documents later on, so this is mainly where you can explain the damage and what you’ve done to repair it. Keep in mind, an item does not have to be in excellent condition for an institution to take it. Items are used in a number of different ways, and sometimes the damage to the item is PART of it’s story! And if repairs or restoration needs to be done on the item, the institutes will hire professionals to bring it back to life. That being said, it is possible that in some cases, the restoration required is more than the institution is willing to take on. Description
Explain what damage has occurred and what repairs are needed. The more detail, the better.

Excellent
Looks brand new and is in excellent working condition. Very little visible wear if any at all. Very well taken care of.
Good
Has minor visible wear and tear, but is still in excellent working condition. Well taken care of.
Okay
Wear and tear is visible on the item(s), but it does not distract from the meaning or story behind the item. Needs some repairs to work properly. No serious damage.
Poor
Wear and tear is visible on the item(s) and distracts from the meaning or story behind the item. Needs extensive repairs to work properly and has endured serious damage.
Damaged
Item is in very rough shape. It is difficult to identify the item because of the damage it has endured. Would require substantial repairs to work properly.

Files

Selected files:

More information:
Feel free to add as many files as you believe are necessary. This can include:
  • Pictures of the Item(s)
  • Ownership Documents
  • Repair Records
  • Record of Authenticity
Some Tips:
  • Please do not include videos or audio files. This tool will create a TXT or PDF document that includes the files you’re adding, so video or audio files will not work. But, be sure to send these items to the institution along with your Donation Resume if you think they’re applicable.
  • Try not to include your personal information, such as address, social security numbers, etc.
  • Make sure the pictures are large, clear, and are free from distracting backgrounds. These do not have to be professional photos, but make sure they showcase your item(s) as best as possible.

Era

More information:

There are a number of dates that might be relevant to your item. Maybe it was made in one decade, but it wasn’t really relevant until decades later. Maybe it was just a plain old mug until Elvis drank out of it in the 1950’s. This is all party of the item’s story, and different dates are significant for different reasons. You will be able to go through the story of the item later on, and that’s where you can put all of the dates and time periods that are a part of your item. If you aren’t sure, that’s okay. Researchers in institutions are experts at dating items. In this section, try to be general.
ex. 1960’s, 13th - 18th Century, November 1987

Helpful Links: Dating Antiques

Location

More information:

Where was the item found? Where was it made? Where did you get it? Usually, a location depends on the story of the item. Is it important because of it’s maker or where it was found? If you aren’t sure, feel free to leave it blank. Specific vs General
The more specific, the better. But what if the item’s importance extends beyond one single location. What if it was popular all over the world? Think about the story of the item, who’s story does it tell? Does it tell the story of a specific neighborhood, city, state, country, continent, or area?
For example, a designer dress may be important because of where it was made, but it could also be important who owned the dress and where they lived.

Subject

More information:

This section is similar to the location section, there is one area for general subject areas and another for specifics. Subject areas are how institutions make sense of their items. Items can have many subject areas that they fall under. This way, if someone is looking for a specific topic, they find as many relevant results as possible. The institution will likely make this decision themselves, but it is helpful for you to give them a basic idea. Specific vs General
The more specific, the better. Think of this like sections in a library. What general section would this item go in? Within that section, what shelf would it fall under?
For example, a collection of Navajo jewelry may have general subjects (Native American History/Culture, Jewelry) and specific subjects (Navajo History 1860-1890, Native American Jewelry Techniques).

Helpful Links: Library of Congress Subject Headings

Story

More information:

When you're describing the item(s) to a friend, what do you tell them? How does this item tell that story? What history does the item have? This is different than the Description section because this is the actual story of the item. The description is more technical, here is where you can be a passionate storyteller. Think of the television show Mysteries at the Museum, there is so much more to an object than a general description of what it is. Interpret it's meaning, tell the institution why it is important.

Helpful Links: Mysteries at the Museum

More information:

Institutions are tasked with keeping their items in pristine condition and protecting them for perpetuity. These questions will help them have a better understanding of the item's condition, especially if it requires restoration. If you want to learn more about how to properly store your items, see the links below.

Materials

More information:

Sometimes identifying materials can be tricky. If you have any information on what metal is used, what kind of paper, or what chemicals have been used on your item(s) please list them here. This is also helpful for institutions because it will help them store the items more securely. A book is pretty simple to store and protect, but if it has metal staples or fasteners, they will need to know about that.

Tax Information

More information:

If you're already donating item(s) to a non-profit institution, why not use it to your advantage? The institution can easily help you draw up documents to prove your donation (a Deed of Gift), however there are a few things that they will not do. Institutions are not legally allowed to give tax advice or appraise your item(s), which you will need for the deduction. And in order for the item(s) to be appraised by a certified appraiser, they may require some kind of authentication. The institution will likely be able to give you a list of appraisers to contact. “Appraisals” are those assessments made regarding the financial value of an object, whether for insurance or fair market value. “Authentications” are those statements made that validate the genuine-ness of a given object, based on the expertise of that individual making the statement.’ While Museum staff are expressly forbidden from providing appraisals on private collections, they do maintain a list of qualified appraisers as a public service.

Helpful Links: Donation Resume Resources: Tax Incentives | IRS: Topic No. 506 Charitable Contributions | IRS: Publication 561, Determining the Value of Donated Property

Ownership

More information:

Every institution will require you to first prove that the item(s) belong to you and that you can legally donate them. If you have any certificates of authenticity, purchase records, titles, or anything else that could prove you own the item, please upload them to the Files section. If you are not able to prove ownership, the institution may be able to help you prove this depending on the circumstances.

Conditions of Donation

More information:

Depending on the institution, you may be able to make certain requests regarding how your item is interpreted and used. For example, if the item is deaccessioned (removed from the collection) you can request that it is returned to you rather than sent to another institution or sold. You may also specify that your name or any other identifying information be left out of the institution's labels and systems. They will still have a record that you donated it, but you will not be referenced anywhere in the item's interpretation. If you have any special requests or concerns, make sure to tell the institution you're working with. But keep in mind that if the item has too many strings attached, they may decide not to take it.

More information:

While most donations are fairly simple, there are certain things that make donating item(s) more challenging. For some types of items, laws may require additional documentation or handling. Your institution will know the procedure for such items, but here is an opportunity to give them a heads-up. For more information on how to handle these kinds of donations, see the links below.
